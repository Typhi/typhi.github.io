Problem: Chasing features over impacts | ⮝ Home

Roadmaps are a useful tool for representing priorities and scheduling work, but we can sometimes end up chasing the roadmap deliverables (ticking off checklists until a feature is complete) instead of going after valuable impacts. We might then ship a feature meeting requirements but not achieving an impact, or make changes and move on to the next item without knowing whether the change was successful or not.

There is value in hitting roadmap milestones and release dates, but this is secondary to our primary task of adding business value.

Consequences of focusing too much on features over impacts

  • Wasted time designing, building and releasing features that achieve little, or nothing, instead of adding value
  • (Worthless) complication of the product which:
    • probable extra inefficiencies for future production
    • makes it harder for users to get value out of your product
  • Frustration for the team

Symptoms

https://cutle.fish/blog/12-signs-youre-working-in-a-feature-factory

Contributing factors

  • Environment mainly incentivizes delivering at certain points, with little attention on results
  • Limited time leads to rushed "ticking off checkboxes" in an attempt to "finish"
  • Minimum Viable Products conceived as minimal functional products, instead of minimum impactful products

How we can try to avoid it

  • Set clear goals about what problem is being addressed and how the solution being worked on will add business value → Introducing changes (2016.11)
    • Communicate this goal clearly and often to external stakeholders and team
    • Check back on this goal frequently to evaluate if it is still the focus of the work being done. It can also happen that a new, more worthy goal has been discovered during design or development.
  • Put focus on measuring the results of change
