Typically, the topic of dividing a development team up into separated working units comes up when the team is getting too large to handle as a single unit. In my experience, this tends to start getting discussed around a total game team size of 15-20, but your mileage _will_ vary depending on the general setup and nature of the work.

Pros of dividing up teams into discrete units

  • It‚Äôs easier to create/maintain valuable working relationships in smaller groups

  • Increases efficiency in some areas: e.g. less people in meetings tends to mean more efficient meetings. Another example: it‚Äôs a convenient structure to silo information so that not everyone (needs to) stay up to date with everything

  • Increased individual responsibility and transparency

  • It provides a framework to use for scaling the team up further

Cons

  • Dividing work by topic area can mean overall priorities are getting addressed less efficiently. Example: imagine we‚Äôve got two topic areas: A and B. Now imagine the priority list by topic looks like this: AABBAA. The team focusing on B will in this example start work on the 3rd most important thing instead of the 2nd.

  • It‚Äôs more difficult for people to have an overview of everything, and this easily leads to all sorts of fun inefficiencies

  • You get some extra overhead to try to mitigate the above. This can take the form of an extra set of sync meetings between pod leaders. Example setup: add an extra daily standup meeting for pod leaders, add a larger pod leader sync per week (potentially just added on to an existing leadership sync), potentially additional syncs on a discipline level (e.g. all server devs get together to talk about ongoing server dev work)

  • You have less flexibility to scale up attention on specific topics if the groups are fixed. Example: If important topic A is suddenly behind schedule, it‚Äôs now harder for various reasons to pull help from the people working on topic B since that‚Äôs _a separate team_.

Things to watch out for when transitioning to a divided team setup

  • Alignment becomes harder to establish and maintain, and subgroups can therefore start pulling in different directions

  • You need to make some decisions about what level of cross-pod knowledge exchange you want to see. Part of the benefit of the split is reducing required communication, but you probably don‚Äôt want to go all the way to 0 either.

  • Human tribalism and unhealthy competition/vibes between pods is a thing that can sprout up over time

  • People don‚Äôt always like being boxed into specific topics! üòÑ

My personal preference:

Because of the cons, in a growing team I like delaying dividing the team as long as reasonable.

When it becomes too unwieldy, I then first prefer to go for a _flexible_ division where pod leaders swap team members around frequently depending on the topics they have coming their way. This IS a little ~fancy though and may be too much complexity for some situations.
