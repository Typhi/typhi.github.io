Exploration

We want to be doing the most valuable thing in pursuit of some goal. In more detail, we want to be generating that value as efficiently as possible, so we actually want to be doing the thing that will generate value most efficiently.

So we collect a list of topics we think might be valuable.

Fueled by:

  • Brainstorming

  • Being inspired by other games

  • Seeing a problem in the numbers

  • Player feedback

  • etc

2

Prioritization

Then we figure out their rough value, and toss that into an ordered list.

Example prioritization decision:

  • Guild Expeditions (5m within a year, but 5 sprints)

  • Overhaul shop (2m within a year, but 1 sprint) ← much more efficient.

Note: determining what is most efficient to be working on can be done with KPI targets, but isn't necessary.

3

Doing stuff

We then work through that list from top to bottom because the list is ordered by efficiency of value generation.

If we were a team of 1, we'd go task by task.

In the case of RoC, we're a team of 30 and cross-functional, so we're often in the situation where we can work on multiple topics at a time, and different people are responsible for doing things at different stages of a task.

We want to know when things are done

So we can plan ahead and keep things running smoothly

  • Example: so we can make sure preparations are ready on time to let feature development start efficiently

  • Example: so that the company can do financial planning

Map the ordered list to a timeline

  • Note: This is fairly straightforward because we already had some rough expectations about how long something will take in order to prioritize it properly

Scheduling comes in 3 varieties. You can plan how long something:

  • Should take: A lot of stuff on the roadmap comes down to this, actually. If we know an impact only makes business sense if it can be done within a certain time, then that's the time we'll schedule for it. If it's actually a lot more complicated then perhaps it doesn't make much business sense to do it, and we could be doing something more valuable with our time. This should of course also be re-evaluated when other thoughts and new information comes in.

  • Will take: This is for the case when you've got something you know you need to do, and also know roughly how much time it will take. A lot of stuff on the roadmap is actually a mix of will and should.

  • Can take: The amount of time we can dedicate to something before something else becomes more urgent. This is something that you will most often see pop up towards the end of sprints or quarters when the next big stuff isn't quite ready yet.

Value optimization

It's not realistic to have 30 cross-functional members working at all times on the single most important thing. For example: Timelines are not 100% trustworthy, and our team isn't perfectly balanced across all disciplines.

So there are holes to slip minor topics into. Make use of those holes.

Example: end of a sprint and half the FE devs and all the BE devs are finishing up something. Overall more efficient to wait with starting half those FE devs on a new topic since they'd get out of sync with the rest of the team. Have them do some minor stuff where they don't bother people focusing on the more important topics and might make something slightly better.

Common traps

  • Getting stuck on one method of exploration

  • Undisciplined prioritization

  • Not frequently working on exploration

  • Not sufficiently examining what value will be generated (e.g. working through a feature checklist)

Answers

How can Product people influence the output and outcomes?

  • Do a good job in stage 1 and 2

  • Frequently re-do Stage 1 and Stage 2

  • Keep Stage 2 and the decisions made there in mind when working on 3

  • Add more people. If the structures can support this, this either speeds up the output or increases the bandwidth.

  • Develop people in the direction that will support valuable output

  • Fire people where the effort of development isn't justified

  • Optimize processes

How is it measured if things are going well?

  • Evaluate feature results, see if they matched what we wanted.

  • Compare our feelings against feelings we've had in the past in similar situations

  • Generally meeting expectations
